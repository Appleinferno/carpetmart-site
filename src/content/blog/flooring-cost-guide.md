"What's it going to cost?" is the first question on everyone's mind, and it's a fair one. The honest answer is that flooring is priced per square foot, and the total depends on the material you choose, how many square feet you're covering, and what it takes to install it. This guide breaks down the pieces so you can budget realistically — and walk into the showroom knowing what to expect.

> A note up front: we don't post fixed prices online because they change with the market and with each job. The numbers below are general ranges to help you plan. For a real figure on your rooms, bring your measurements by — estimates are always free.

## The three parts of any flooring price

Every flooring project has three cost components:

1. **The material** — the floor itself, priced per square foot.
2. **Installation** — labor to install it, also usually per square foot.
3. **Prep and extras** — tearing out the old floor, hauling it away, subfloor repair, transition strips, and trim.

People often price out only the first one and get surprised by the other two. A good quote includes all three.

## Material costs by flooring type

Here's roughly how the materials stack up, from most budget-friendly to most premium:

- **[Laminate](/flooring/laminate):** Among the lowest-cost options. Great wood looks for a small budget, with a tough surface. Higher AC-rated (more durable) lines cost more.
- **[Carpet](/flooring/carpet):** Wide range. Builder-grade carpet is one of the cheapest ways to cover a big room; premium nylon and pet-proof lines cost more. Don't forget the pad.
- **[Vinyl plank](/flooring/vinyl-plank):** Mid-range and the best value for many homes. Entry-level planks are affordable; thick rigid-core planks with a heavy wear layer cost more — and are worth it in high-traffic and rental settings.
- **[Vinyl flooring](/flooring/vinyl-flooring):** One of the most economical floors. Seamless and fully waterproof, it's a budget-friendly pick for bathrooms, laundry rooms, utility spaces and rentals.
- **[Hardwood](/flooring/hardwood):** The premium option. Costs the most up front, but lasts for decades and can be refinished — and it adds resale value.

## Installation costs

Installation is priced by the complexity of the work, not just the square footage:

- **Carpet** is usually the quickest and lowest-cost install — often a single day for a whole house.
- **Floating floors (vinyl plank and laminate)** are moderate; click-lock planks go down efficiently over a prepped subfloor.
- **Glue-down vinyl plank and hardwood** take more labor.
- **Vinyl flooring** is cut and fit in large, seamless pieces, so labor is usually quick and economical.

You can also save on labor by buying **material-only** and installing it yourself or using your own contractor — we sell both ways.

## The costs people forget

These are the line items that surprise people, so budget for them:

- **Tear-out and disposal** of the old flooring. Pulling up old carpet is cheap; busting out old tile is not.
- **Subfloor prep or repair.** If the subfloor is uneven, damaged or squeaky, fixing it first is money well spent — it determines how good (and how quiet) the finished floor is.
- **Transitions and trim.** Thresholds between rooms, stair nosings, quarter-round and baseboard all add up on a whole-house job.
- **Furniture moving and appliance disconnects**, if needed.

## How to get an accurate number

The fastest way to a real price is to come in with three things:

1. **Square footage** of each room (length × width; round up).
2. **What's there now** and whether it needs to come out.
3. **A rough idea of the look** you're after — we'll show you options at different price points.

From there we can put together a quote that covers material, installation and prep, so there are no surprises.
